Undergraduate Research Project

In Silico Studies of Bioactive Compounds of
Azadirachta indica on Phospholipase A2

University of Cape Coast

Department of Biochemistry · Biochemistry Laboratory
Advisor: Professor Michael B. Adinotey  ·  November 2022

Collaborated with a team of researchers in conducting a comprehensive study investigating in silico studies of bioactive compounds of Azadirachta indica on phospholipase A2 as a potential target for snakebite treatment.

Designed and executed experiments to analyze the potential of some phytochemicals from Azadirachta indica as inhibitors of the phospholipase A2 (PLA2) protein found in snake venom — using bioinformatics tools to interpret results and present findings at the Department of Biochemistry, earning recognition for the project's innovation and potential impact.

Advisor: Prof. Michael B. Adinotey

01
Background & Rationale

Azadirachta indica (Neem) is a medicinal plant well known for its broad pharmacological properties. Snakebite envenomation remains a neglected tropical disease causing significant mortality globally, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. Phospholipase A2 (PLA2) enzymes present in snake venom are key mediators of tissue damage, inflammation, and toxicity.

This study hypothesized that bioactive phytochemicals from neem could inhibit PLA2 activity through molecular binding, potentially offering a complementary or alternative therapeutic approach to conventional antivenom treatment.

02
Methodology & Tools
  • Retrieved PLA2 protein structures from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) and prepared them for docking using AutoDock Vina
  • Identified and downloaded bioactive compounds of Azadirachta indica from PubChem, including azadirachtin, nimbin, nimbolide, gedunin, and quercetin
  • Used PyRx for virtual screening — minimizing energy of ligands and generating docking grids
  • Analyzed binding affinities, hydrogen bond interactions, and hydrophobic contacts using LigPlot+ and PyMol
  • Calculated ADMET properties using Swiss-ADME and SwissTargetPrediction to evaluate drug-likeness
  • Validated results against known PLA2 inhibitors as reference compounds

03
Results & Key Findings

Several bioactive compounds from Azadirachta indica demonstrated promising binding affinities to the PLA2 active site. The top-ranked compounds exhibited favorable interactions with key catalytic residues, comparable to or better than known inhibitors.

  • Nimbolide showed the highest binding affinity, with strong hydrogen bond interactions with catalytic residues His48 and Asp49
  • Gedunin demonstrated excellent ADMET properties alongside strong binding scores
  • All top candidates satisfied Lipinski's Rule of Five, indicating strong oral bioavailability potential
  • Results suggest these compounds merit further in vitro and in vivo validation as anti-snakebite agents

05
Conclusion & Future Directions

This study provides computational evidence that bioactive compounds from Azadirachta indica have the potential to inhibit PLA2 enzymes in snake venom. These findings lay a foundation for future wet-lab validation, including enzyme assay-based inhibition studies and in vivo animal model trials.

As a continuation, I am interested in extending this work to include molecular dynamics simulations to assess binding stability over time, and to explore synergistic combinations of neem compounds for enhanced anti-venom efficacy. This research exemplifies the power of computational approaches in accelerating drug discovery for neglected tropical diseases.
